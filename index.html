Detailed Study Notes on Carbohydrates

General Information about Carbohydrates

Definition:

  • The general formula of carbohydrates is (CH2O)n, where n is greater than or equal to 3. They are also known as hydrated carbon.

  • The value of n usually ranges from 3 to 6, but it can go up to 9.

  • Many carbohydrates possess more intricate chemical formulas due to the presence of groups like amino, phosphate, and sulfate.

Carbohydrate Cycle in Nature:

  • The primary source of carbohydrates in nature is photosynthesis by plants.

Types of Carbohydrates

  1. Monosaccharides: Composed of a single sugar monomer.

  2. Oligosaccharides: Consist of two to ten sugar monomers linked by glycosidic bonds.

    • There is some ambiguity in the definition of oligosaccharides. Some textbooks define disaccharides as having two sugar monomers, while others classify molecules with 3 to 20 sugar monomers as oligosaccharides.

  3. Polysaccharides: Made up of ten or more sugar monomers.

Monosaccharides: Aldoses and Ketoses

Structure: Monosaccharides consist of aldehydes or ketones with multiple hydroxyl groups (polyhydroxy).

  • Aldoses: The carbonyl group is located at the end of the carbon chain.

  • Ketoses: The carbonyl group is not located at the end of the carbon chain.

Nomenclature:

  • The root of the name indicates the number of carbon atoms in the molecule.

  • For example, in Aldo-tri-ose, "tri" signifies a three-carbon sugar.

Simplest Monosaccharides: The simplest monosaccharides are three-carbon sugars: glyceraldehyde and dihydroxyacetone.

  • Dihydroxyacetone is unique among sugars because it lacks a chiral center, meaning it doesn't have D-Form and L-Form isomers.

Interconversion: Aldoses and ketoses can be interconverted through enzyme-catalyzed reactions.

Stereochemistry of Monosaccharides

Fischer Projection:

  • Definition: A representation where all horizontal bonds point towards the observer and vertical bonds point away.

  • Fischer projection is commonly used to depict carbohydrates.

  • D-Form & L-Form: In Fischer projection, if the hydroxyl group (-OH) on the chiral center farthest from the highest oxidized carbon (aldehyde or ketone) is on the right, it's the D-Form. If it's on the left, it's the L-Form.

  • Most carbohydrates in living organisms exist in the physiologically active D-Form, while amino acids are primarily in the L-Form.

  • Fischer projection helps visualize structural differences and conversions between molecules.

Haworth Projection:

  • Cyclization: When the number of carbons (n) is 5 or more, monosaccharides in solution predominantly exist in a cyclic form (cyclization).

  • Haworth projection is commonly used to represent cyclic monosaccharides.

Features of Haworth Projection:

  • Carbon atoms are not explicitly shown.

  • Hydrogen atoms attached to carbons are not depicted.

  • Thick lines represent bonds closer to the observer.

  • Substituents below the ring in Haworth projection correspond to substituents on the right side in Fischer projection ("H below = F right").

Nomenclature of 4- and 5-Carbon Ketoses: The names of 4- and 5-carbon ketoses are derived by adding "ul" to the middle of the corresponding 4- and 5-carbon aldose names.
For example: D-Erythrose → D-Erythrulose

Epimers

Definition: Two sugars that differ in configuration at only one carbon atom.

Examples:

  • D-Mannose and D-Glucose are epimers at C-2.

  • D-Galactose and D-Glucose are epimers at C-4.

  • D-Mannose and D-Galactose are not epimers because they differ at both C-2 and C-4.

Calculating the Number of Epimers: The formula for calculating the number of epimers is 2^(n-3) for aldoses and 2^(n-4) for ketoses, where 'n' represents the number of carbon atoms in the sugar. This formula only considers epimers of a single D-form and excludes the chirality of the last carbon atom.

Relationship: Epimers are diastereomers, not enantiomers.

Interconversion: Epimers can be interconverted by enzymes called epimerases. D-galactose requires conversion to D-glucose by epimerase for metabolism.

Cyclization of Monosaccharides

Chemical Principle: Cyclization of common monosaccharides (n ≥ 5) involves the formation of a hemiacetal or hemiketal.

  • Aldose Cyclization: The hydroxyl group on carbon 5 attacks the aldehyde group on carbon 1, forming a hemiacetal.

  • Ketose Cyclization: The hydroxyl group on carbon 5 attacks the ketone group on carbon 2, forming a hemiketal.

Examples:

  • Glucose cyclization results in a six-membered ring compound called pyranose.

  • Fructose cyclization yields a five-membered ring compound called furanose.

Anomers

Definition: Specific type of epimer where interconversion (mutarotation) is a natural equilibrium process in aqueous solutions. Other epimer interconversions require enzymes.

  • Anomeric Carbon: The carbon atom in the ring that forms the hemiacetal or hemiketal (bonded to the oxygen and has the lower number).

  • α-Form: The -OH on the anomeric carbon is on the opposite side of the ring plane as the C-6.

  • β-Form: The -OH on the anomeric carbon is on the same side of the ring plane as the C-6.

Mutarotation

  • α-Form and β-Form can interconvert in solution by opening to the linear form and then re-cyclizing. The β-Form is more prevalent (~66%) due to less steric hindrance and lower energy.

  • Linear glucose, though only 1% in solution, is the primary form used in glucose metabolism.

  • Mutarotation involves bond breaking and is a configurational change, not a conformational change (folding).

Chair and Boat Forms

  • Six-carbon sugars can exist in boat or chair conformations.

  • The chair form is more stable due to greater distance between hydroxyl groups, resulting in lower energy.

  • The two chair forms shown in the source are conformational isomers, not anomers or epimers. Anomers and epimers are configurational isomers.

Stereoisomer Terminology

  • Enantiomer: Mirror images of each other, with all chiral centers having opposite configurations.

  • Diastereomer: Stereoisomers that are not enantiomers.

  • Epimer: Differ in configuration at only one carbon atom.

  • Anomer: Cyclic sugars where the hydroxyl group on the anomeric carbon is on opposite sides (α) or the same side (β) of the Haworth projection plane as the C-5 hydroxyl group. Can be considered a special type of epimer.

Derivatives and Nomenclature of Carbohydrates

Derivatives of Carbohydrates:

  • Amino Sugars: An -OH group on the main chain is replaced by an -NH2 group. The names end in "-samine".

    • Examples: β-D-Mannosamine, β-D-Galactosamine (components of bacterial cell walls)

    • The substitution usually occurs at C-2.

  • Deoxy Sugars: Any -OH group on the sugar is replaced by an -H.

    • Examples: β-L-Fucose (found in glycolipids and glycoproteins), α-L-Rhamnose (common in plant polysaccharides)

    • The substitution usually occurs at C-6.

  • Sugar Phosphates: An -OH group on the main chain is replaced by a phosphate group (phosphorylation).

  • Acidic Sugars: Sugars containing -COOH or -COO- groups. These are negatively charged and found in seaweed. They are usually formed by oxidation of the aldehyde group. At pH 7, they exist mostly in ionized form (carboxylates).

  • Aldonic Acids: The aldehyde group on the sugar is oxidized to a carboxylic acid. Glucose oxidized in this way is specifically called gluconic acid.

  • Uronic Acids: The terminal carbon (C-6) of the carbon chain is oxidized to a carboxylic acid.

  • Lactones: Stable intramolecular esters formed by aldonic acids and uronic acids.

  • Sialic Acids: Acidic sugars with a nine-carbon backbone. The term "sialic acid" can also refer specifically to N-acetylneuraminic acid, the most important member.

Many carbohydrate modifications occur at C-2, but there are exceptions. Sialic acid is modified at C-5.

Nomenclature and Abbreviations of Carbohydrates

Abbreviations:

  • "N" signifies an amine group.

  • "Ac" represents an acetyl group.

  • "A" denotes an acid.

  • "NAc" indicates N-linked acetylation (acetylation of an amine group).

  • The abbreviation order is the reverse of the order in the full name.

  • Most sugar abbreviations use the first three letters of their full name. However, glucose is abbreviated as "Glc" to avoid confusion with amino acid abbreviations.

  • The source provides a table of common sugar abbreviations.

Reducing Sugars: Sugars capable of reducing Cu2+ ions.

Detection of Monosaccharides

Fehling's Reagent:

  • Composition: A blue complex containing copper ions (Cu2+).

  • Principle: Monosaccharides act as reducing sugars and are oxidized, simultaneously reducing Cu2+ to Cu2O. The aldehyde group is primarily responsible for this reducing ability.

  • Example: Glucose is oxidized to gluconic acid.

  • Only linear sugars are measured, but due to dynamic equilibrium, the test doesn't just detect the 1% present in linear form.

  • Aldehydes can be oxidized to carboxylic acids by Cu2+ in a strong alkaline environment, but ketones cannot.

  • All monosaccharides (aldoses and ketoses) are reducing sugars and can cause a color change in Fehling's reagent. While ketones are not inherently reducing, ketoses are alpha-hydroxy ketones, which can tautomerize to form aldehydes in aqueous solution, giving them reducing properties.

Enzymatic Method for Blood Glucose Measurement:

  • Steps:

    • Glucose oxidase oxidizes glucose to gluconic acid, producing H2O2 as a byproduct.

    • Peroxidase uses H2O2 to oxidize o-dianisidine, forming a stable, colored product.

  • This method involves two oxidation-reduction reactions, catalyzed by glucose oxidase and peroxidase.

Advantages over Fehling's Reagent:

  • Quantitative analysis.

  • Specificity (Fehling's reagent cannot differentiate glucose from other reducing sugars in solution).

Glycated Hemoglobin (HbA1c) Method for Blood Glucose Measurement:

  • Rationale: Single-point glucose measurements can fluctuate and be influenced by factors like fasting. HbA1c provides an average blood glucose level over time.

  • Principle:

    • Red blood cell proteins equilibrate glucose concentration inside and outside the cell.

    • Glycation (nonenzymatic reaction) of hemoglobin occurs in proportion to blood glucose levels.

  • Glucose reacts with amino acids in hemoglobin (Val's amino-terminal or Lys's ε-amino group) to form glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c).

  • Advantages: Less susceptible to short-term fluctuations and reflects blood glucose control over 1-2 months (red blood cell lifespan is ~120 days).

Disadvantages: Lacks specificity (not only glucose reacts with hemoglobin) and doesn't reflect real-time blood glucose levels.

Normal HbA1c range is 3.5% to 5.6%.
Prediabetes is indicated by HbA1c levels between 5.7% and 6.4%.

Physiological Roles of Monosaccharides

  • Fuel: Glucose is the primary source of ATP production in cells. Other monosaccharides can be converted to energy, but less efficiently, as they often need to be metabolized into glucose first.

  • Building Blocks: Monosaccharides can link to other molecules via N-glycosidic bonds. For example, they form nucleosides with nitrogenous bases in nucleotides.

Disaccharides, Polysaccharides, Glycoconjugates, and Glycomics

Disaccharides

  • Common in plants.

  • The three common disaccharides are sucrose, lactose, and maltose. The enzymes that break them down are sucrase, lactase, and maltase, respectively.

  • Sucrose is the sweetest of the three, while lactose is the least sweet.

  • Sucrose is the most abundant disaccharide.

Formation:

  • Formed by two monosaccharides linked by a covalent O-glycosidic bond.

  • The reaction that joins two monosaccharides can be viewed as a nucleophilic reaction between one monosaccharide acting as a hemiacetal (electrophile) and the other as an alcohol (nucleophile).

  • Glycosidic bonds are acetal or ketal bonds formed by the condensation between the -OH of a cyclic monosaccharide hemiacetal (or hemiketal) and the -OH, -NH2, or -SH of another molecule, such as an alcohol, sugar, purine, or pyrimidine.

Maltose:

  • Formed by the dehydration condensation of two glucose molecules.

  • Its name can be written as α-D-glucopyranosyl-(1→4)-D-glucopyranoside, where (1→4) indicates that C-1 of one Glc is linked to C-4 of the other Glc, and α indicates that the substituents on C-1 and C-5 of the Glc at the non-reducing end are in the trans configuration. The reducing end Glc is not labeled because it can exist in both α- and β-forms in solution. Maltose can also be written as Glc(α1→4)Glc.

Sucrose:

  • Formed by the dehydration condensation of one glucose molecule and one fructose molecule.

  • Its name can be written as α-D-glucopyranosyl-β-D-fructofuranoside or Glc(α1↔2β)Fru ≡ Fru(2β↔α1)Glc. The order of Glc and Fru can be reversed because there is no reducing end.

  • Both Glc and Fru provide anomeric carbon for condensation.

  • Sucrose is a non-reducing sugar because it is not easily oxidized. Therefore, it is suitable for storing energy.

  • Sucrose is synthesized in the cytoplasm by sucrose 6-phosphate synthase and sucrose 6-phosphate phosphatase.

Lactose:

  • Formed by the dehydration condensation of one glucose molecule and one galactose molecule.

  • Its name can be written as β-D-galactopyranosyl-(1→4)-β-D-glucopyranose or Gal(β1→4)Glc.

  • When the name does not specify L or D form, it is the D form.

  • Maltose and lactose are reducing sugars because they have a reducing end, while sucrose does not.

  • Maltose and lactose can undergo mutarotation, but sucrose cannot.

Changing the ending of the English names of the three disaccharides from -ose to -ase gives the name of the corresponding degrading enzyme.

Sweet Taste Receptors

  • Natural sugars such as glucose, sucrose, and fructose can bind to both T1R2 and T1R3, while artificial sweeteners bind only to T1R2.

  • T1R2 and T1R3 form a heterodimer that functions together.

  • Sweetness is measured relative to a standard of 0.5% sucrose, with the subject judging the sweetness of the sample as a multiple of the standard.

  • Sweet taste receptors vary among species.

  • The receptor is composed of an extracellular domain, a cysteine-rich domain (CRD), a membrane domain with 7 transmembrane helices, and a signaling receptor.

Polysaccharides

  • Unlike proteins, polysaccharides do not have a fixed molecular weight because there is no fixed template during their synthesis and no definite stopping point.

  • Polysaccharides are also known as glycans and are composed of at least 10 monosaccharides.

Homopolysaccharides

  • Composed of a single type of monosaccharide.

  • Used for energy storage, such as glycogen, or as structural support, such as cellulose.

  • Can be linear (unbranched) or branched.

Starch:

  • Composed of D-glucose and is the form of energy storage in plants.

  • Includes amylose (linear starch) and amylopectin (branched starch).

  • The ratio of amylose to amylopectin in ordinary starch granules is 20% and 80%, respectively.

  • Usually stored in cells in the form of granules.

  • Amylose is hydrolyzed and digested more slowly than amylopectin, but as an energy storage substance, it occupies less space. Therefore, in ordinary starch granules, amylose (α1→4) is tightly intertwined with the helical structure of the long chains of amylopectin (α1→6).

Amylose:

  • D-glucose is linked by (α1→4)-linkages.

  • α-helical amylose can retain water in the center of the helix. Enzymes can break it down from the long chain, so α-helical amylose is more diverse than the tight structure of cellulose.

Amylopectin:

  • Multiple amylose molecules are joined by (α1→6)-linkages to form branches.

  • Branching occurs every 24-30 residues.

  • The more branches, the more space between chains, resulting in better water absorption. Cells need another set of enzymes to break down branches, so theoretically, it is also more stable than amylose.

Glycogen:

  • Composed of D-glucose and is the form of energy storage in animals.

  • Linked by (α1→4)-linkages, with branches formed by (α1→6)-linkages.

  • Exists in the form of granules in the liver and muscles, and its structure and condensation method are similar to those of starch.

  • Branches every 8-12 residues, which is more compact than starch (every 24-30 residues).

  • Glycogen has a higher branching density than starch, so when it is used as an energy molecule, enzymes can act on the ends of different branches simultaneously, resulting in higher regulatory efficiency. This is why animals choose to store energy in the form of glycogen.

  • Energy is not stored in the form of glucose monomers because it would cause excessive osmotic pressure in cells.

  • Each branch in glycogen ends in a non-reducing sugar unit, so a glycogen molecule containing n branches contains n+1 non-reducing ends (n branches + main chain).

Cellulose:

  • Composed of D-glucose linked by (β1→4)-linkages to form a planar structure and is the main component of plant cell walls.

  • The parallel long chains of cellulose are linked by hydrogen bonds. Because the hydrogen bonds form internally and do not interact with water, cellulose is not easily soluble in water.

  • Multiple enzymes are required to break the hydrogen bonds in cellulose, so only a few organisms in nature, such as bacteria and microorganisms, can break it down (e.g., wood fungi). Cows, sheep, and termites have symbiotic microorganisms (Trichonympha) in their intestines, so they can obtain energy from wood and grass.

  • α-amylases and glycosidases can hydrolyze the (α1→4) bonds in molecules such as starch and glycogen, but humans lack the enzymes to hydrolyze (β1→4) bonds and therefore cannot break down cellulose.

Chitin:

  • Composed of N-acetyl-D-glucosamine linked by (β1→4)-linkages to form a linear polysaccharide. Note the alternating carbon sequence of the monomer in the figure below. It is commonly found in the exoskeletons of arthropods and fungi.

  • Cellulose and chitin are both structural polysaccharides. The only difference is that chitin has an acetylated amino group at C-2 instead of the hydroxyl group in cellulose.

  • In general, polysaccharides with (α1→4)-linkages have storage functions, while those with (β1→4)-linkages have support functions.

  • Amylose forms a helical structure with glucose linked by (α1→4).

  • Cellulose forms a planar structure with glucose linked by (β1→4).

Heteropolysaccharides

  • Composed of two or more types of monosaccharides.

  • Usually serve as extracellular support structures. Their composition can be two monomers alternating or three or more different monomers. They can be further divided into two categories based on the presence or absence of branches.

Peptidoglycan:

  • Commonly found in bacterial cell walls.

  • Both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria have peptidoglycan in their cell walls. Gram-positive bacteria have a thick layer of peptidoglycan that forms a strong complex with crystal violet and is stained.

  • Mainly composed of the disaccharides N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc, NAG) and N-acetylmuramic acid (Mur2Ac, NAM) linked by (β1→4)-linkages.

  • The disaccharides form unbranched chains that are linked by peptides. The peptides are further linked by pentaglycine cross-links to help make the structure more compact.

  • Because the peptides in peptidoglycan contain both L- and D-form amino acids, and many animals, including humans, usually only process L-form amino acids, it is difficult to break down this structure.

Agarose:

  • Extracted from seaweed and serves as one of the supporting structures of the cell wall, protecting the seaweed.

Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)

  • Found in the extracellular matrix.

  • Composed of long, unbranched chains of repeating disaccharide units, where the disaccharide is composed of an amino sugar (N-acetylglucosamine or N-acetylgalactosamine) and galactose or a uronic sugar (glucuronic acid or iduronic acid).

  • Most GAGs are sulfated and therefore have a high density of negative charges. This makes them highly soluble and able to retain water, forming a gel-like substance that protects tissues from impact damage. In addition, when deformed by impact, they can recover quickly due to electrostatic repulsion.

  • Usually, the C-6 of one monosaccharide is oxidized to COO-, the C-2 of the other monosaccharide is modified with NHCOCH3, and the hydroxyl group on C-6 is sulfated.

  • GAGs exhibit high heterogeneity in molecular weight, disaccharide structure, and sulfation because their synthesis is not template-driven like proteins or nucleic acids, but is simply determined by the synthesizing enzymes.

Heparin:

  • The diversity of heparin is due to the different number of sulfates added by sulfation. It is a natural anticoagulant found in many body tissues.

  • Heparin is the most negatively charged biomolecule known, surpassing DNA.

  • The -OH groups of heparin are extensively sulfated and carry a large negative charge, so clotting factors are easily adsorbed by it and cannot exert their clotting effect. It is widely used as a clinical anticoagulant.

  • Heparin can bind strongly to the anticoagulant antithrombin III, and this complex can inhibit thrombin. If it is overly inhibited, positively charged substances need to be added to antagonize it.

  • Heparin is a highly sulfated GAG linked by (α1→4) bonds to form disaccharide units, and the disaccharides are linked by (α1→4) bonds to form polysaccharides.

GAGs require precursors and enzymes to polymerize, and the quantity must be sufficient. Taking commercially available glucosamine supplements only provides precursors, and the lack of enzymes limits their effectiveness.

Important Sugar Bond Summary

  • (α1→4): Amylose, glycogen, heparin

  • (α1→6): Amylopectin, glycogen

  • (β1→4): Chitin, cellulose, peptidoglycan

Glycoconjugates

Glycoconjugates are a general term for substances produced by the covalent binding of carbohydrates to chemical substances such as proteins, peptide chains, lipids, and carbohydrates. Examples include glycoproteins, glycopeptides, peptidoglycans, glycolipids, and lipopolysaccharides.

  • The carbohydrate must be at least an oligosaccharide, not a monosaccharide.

  • Carbohydrates often play a decisive role in the entire conjugate. For example, the detailed structure of carbohydrates on certain proteins in the blood can determine whether they bind to cells or determine the lifespan of the protein.

  • Glycoconjugates are involved in cell-cell recognition, cell-matrix interactions, and detoxification processes.

  • Substances such as DNA, RNA, ATP, cAMP, cGMP, NADH, NADPH, and CoA are all combinations of carbohydrates and other chemical substances, but they are not classified as glycoconjugates. Glycoconjugates usually refer to macromolecules.

Common Glycoconjugates

Proteoglycans:

  • Proteoglycans refer to glycosaminoglycan (GAG) chains covalently bound to a core protein (proteoglycan = core protein + GAG).

  • The proportion of GAGs in proteoglycans can be as high as 90% (by weight).

  • Proteoglycans can form membrane proteins or aggregate into the extracellular matrix.

  • The terminal end of the sugar chain (attached to the protein) in proteoglycans is the monosaccharide sequence (Gal-Gal-Xyl), and the anomeric carbon of xylose forms an O-glycosidic bond with the OH group on serine.

  • The serine to which GAGs are attached always has the sequence Gly-X-Gly-Ser around it, where X is any amino acid other than proline.

  • In the textbook "Lehninger Principles of Biochemistry 7e," GlcA and Gal-Gal-Xyl in the box in the figure above are collectively referred to as the tetrasaccharide bridge.

  • Syndecans (see figure below) have a transmembrane domain and an extracellular domain, with 3-5 GAGs (heparan sulfate chains or chondroitin sulfate chains) linked to the protein.

  • Because GAGs carry many negative charges, they repel each other rather than becoming entangled when impacted. Therefore, proteins carrying GAGs can recover quickly.

  • GAGs rarely exist alone and usually cross-link with proteins.

Glycoproteins:

  • A protein containing oligosaccharide chains, with the two linked by covalent bonds. The oligosaccharide chains are usually attached to the protein during the modification process of glycosylation and typically have 3-20 sugars. The mass ratio of carbohydrates can range from 1% to 70%.

Protein glycosylation:

  • The process of attaching sugar molecules (e.g., fructose or glucose) to protein molecules without enzymatic control. The process of attaching sugars to proteins under enzymatic control is called glycosylation.

  • Occurs in the Golgi apparatus. The Golgi apparatus is the sorting center for proteins, with the cis face receiving vesicles from the endoplasmic reticulum and the trans face sending vesicles to their target locations.

Glycosphingolipid

  • ABO blood type is determined by the different oligosaccharides attached to glycolipids on the red blood cell membrane.

  • Different sugar groups are attached through different glycosyltransferases. Therefore, differences in attached sugars also affect disease occurrence. For example, people with blood type O are more susceptible to Helicobacter pylori infection.

Basic Structure of Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)

  • LPS is composed of three regions: Lipid A, core sugars, and O-antigen.

  • Core sugars are key to triggering immune responses.

  • O-antigen contains repeating oligosaccharide units.

Physiological Reactions of Glycoconjugates

  • Carbohydrates can also be signaling molecules and assist in many reactions in organisms.

  • Lectins are proteins with high affinity and specificity for carbohydrates on glycoproteins. They can bind to carbohydrates on the cell surface and interact with them. Galectin-3C in the figure below is a member of the lectin family.

  • Galectin-3 is a glycoprotein encoded by the LGALS3 gene in humans. It has an affinity for β-galactoside and exhibits antibacterial activity against bacteria and fungi.

  • Selectins are lectins on the plasma membrane involved in cell recognition, connection, and adhesion.

Influenza Viral Lectin

(mnemonic: H—Holds sialic acid/N—Nicks (cleaves) sialic acid)

  • H (virus hemagglutinin protein): There are 18 types.

  • Influenza viruses use H to recognize oligosaccharides expressed on the host cell membrane, thereby attaching to host cells for infection.

  • N (viral neuraminidase = viral sialidase): There are 11 types.

  • This binding site can recognize sialic acid (N-acetylneuraminic acid) and antiviral drugs.

  • It cleaves oligosaccharides (terminal sialic acid residues of sugars) connected to the host through sialidase, releasing the virus to expand the scope of infection.

  • The influenza drugs oseltamivir (Tamiflu) and zanamivir (Relenza) are "molecules similar to sialic acid residues" that can competitively inhibit the interaction between sialic acid residues on sugars and sialidase, reducing the release of viruses from infected cells through budding.

H. pylori Lectin

  • Helicobacter pylori, the only bacterium that can survive in the human stomach, recognizes the epithelial cells of the gastric lining and alters the environment in the stomach, causing gastritis, gastric ulcers, and gastric cancer.

  • People with blood type O are more susceptible to infection. This is thought to be because the lectin binds 7-8 times more strongly to oligosaccharides on their epithelial cells than to those of other blood types.

Glycomics

Definition

  • Glycomics is the systematic study of all carbohydrates in a given cell type or organism, including those attached to proteins and lipids (glycoproteins, glycolipids).

  • To establish the complete structure of oligosaccharides and polysaccharides, it is necessary to determine their linear sequence, branch positions, monosaccharide configurations, and glycosidic linkages. This is more complex than genomics and proteomics.

Detailed Study Notes on Glucose Metabolism and Glycolysis

These study notes provide an in-depth explanation of glucose metabolism, focusing on glycolysis, a fundamental biochemical pathway.

Metabolism of Carbohydrates

  • Metabolism, the sum of all chemical reactions within a living organism, is crucial for energy production.

Metabolic Pathways

  • Each substance follows distinct metabolic pathways that can influence each other.

  • Catabolism: Breaking down large molecules (polymers) into smaller molecules (monomers).

  • Anabolism: Synthesizing complex molecules (polymers) from simpler ones (monomers).

Glucose Metabolism

Importance of Glucose: Glucose is the primary energy source for organisms and a precursor for DNA and RNA synthesis.

Metabolic Pathways:

  • Structural Polymer Synthesis: Glucose is essential for synthesizing structural polymers like cellulose in plant cell walls.

  • Energy Storage: Glucose is stored as energy reserves in the form of glycogen, starch, and sucrose.

  • Pentose Phosphate Pathway: This pathway utilizes glucose to synthesize the pentose sugars required for DNA and RNA.

  • Glycolysis: A fundamental metabolic pathway that breaks down glucose, providing insights into ATP and NAD+ functions.

Glycolysis: An Overview

Definition: A series of reactions that convert one molecule of glucose (6 carbons) into two molecules of pyruvate (3 carbons).

Location: Occurs in the cytoplasm of the cell.

Oxygen Dependence: Can occur in both aerobic (with oxygen) and anaerobic (without oxygen) conditions.

Phases: Glycolysis is divided into two main phases:

  • Investment Phase (Steps 1-5): Requires an input of energy in the form of ATP.

  • Payoff Phase (Steps 6-10): Generates ATP and NADH.

Glycolysis: Detailed Step-by-Step Breakdown

I. Investment Phase

  1. Step 1: Phosphorylation of Glucose

    • Reaction: Glucose is phosphorylated to glucose-6-phosphate (G6P) using one ATP molecule.

    • This reaction is irreversible and ensures that glucose remains trapped inside the cell.

    • Enzyme: Hexokinase or Glucokinase.

      • Hexokinase (I-III): Found in most tissues; high affinity for glucose, ensuring efficient glucose phosphorylation even at low blood sugar levels. Inhibited by its product, G6P.

      • Glucokinase (Hexokinase IV): Primarily found in the liver; lower affinity for glucose, becoming active only at higher glucose concentrations. If glucokinase mutates, it can lead to type 2 diabetes, showing a strong connection between glucokinase and blood glucose regulation.

    • Key Concepts:

      • Irreversible Reactions: Identified by the inability to produce the initial substrate from the product using the same enzyme.

      • Induced Fit: Hexokinase undergoes a conformational change upon binding to glucose, facilitating the phosphorylation reaction.

      • Cofactor: Mg2+ stabilizes ATP in the hexokinase active site.

    • Significance of Phosphorylation:

      • Prevents glucose from leaving the cell.

      • Increases the molecule's reactivity, → subsequent steps.

    • Kinase: Transfers a phosphate group from a nucleoside triphosphate (such as ATP) to a substrate. Examples include:

      • Hexokinase and glucokinase, which phosphorylate sugars.

      • Glycogen phosphorylase kinase, which phosphorylates proteins.

      • Nucleoside diphosphate kinase, which transfers phosphate between nucleotides.

      • PEP carboxykinase, which catalyzes phosphorylation of metabolic intermediates.

  2. Step 2: Conversion of G6P to F6P

    • Reaction: Glucose-6-phosphate (G6P) is isomerized to fructose-6-phosphate (F6P).

    • Reversible reaction.

    • Enzyme: Phosphohexose Isomerase.

    • Requires Mg2+ as a cofactor.

    • Key Concepts:

      • Isomerization: Rearrangement of atoms within a molecule without changing its chemical formula. (More symmetrical)

      • Purpose: Conversion to fructose facilitates the subsequent cleavage step.

    • Important Protein Enzymes in the Reaction:

      • Histidine is responsible for opening and closing the ring structure. Sugars with more than five carbons tend to form a cyclic structure, so only about 1% of glucose exists in the linear form in solution. Histidine donates a hydrogen, opening the ring to form a linear chain, which allows for the aldose-ketose transformation. Afterward, the ring closes again.

      • Glutamic acid is responsible for the aldose-ketose transformation. It transfers a hydrogen from C-2 to C-1, facilitating the conversion of glucose-6-phosphate (G6P) into fructose-6-phosphate (F6P).

  3. Step 3: Phosphorylation of F6P to FBP

    • Reaction: Fructose-6-phosphate (F6P) is phosphorylated to fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (FBP) using one ATP molecule.

    • Irreversible reaction and a key regulatory point in glycolysis.

    • Enzyme: Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1).

    • Key Concepts:

      • Committed Step: Once FBP is formed, it is committed to the glycolytic pathway.

      • Allosteric Regulation: PFK-1 activity is tightly regulated by:

        • Inhibitors: High levels of ATP and citrate signal energy sufficiency and inhibit PFK-1.

        • Activators: High levels of AMP and ADP indicate low energy states and activate PFK-1.

    • Significance: This step ensures that glucose is committed to glycolysis when energy levels are low.

  4. Step 4: Cleavage of FBP

    • Reaction: Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (FBP) is cleaved into two 3-carbon molecules: dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P or GAP).

    • Reversible reaction.

    • Enzyme: Aldolase.

    • Key Concepts:

      • Aldol Cleavage: A common reaction in carbohydrate metabolism, breaking down a sugar molecule.

      • The enzyme Aldolase uses Aspartate and Lysine residues to facilitate acid-base catalysis during the reaction.

    • Significance: This step creates two 3-carbon molecules that can be further processed to extract energy.

    • The reaction mechanism:

      • The linear form of the substrate binds to the enzyme’s lysine residue, forming a covalent bond through a nucleophilic attack.

      • An acidic group (A) provides a proton (H+), and dehydration occurs, forming a protonated Schiff base (a type of imine containing a C=N bond).

      • A basic group (B) abstracts the proton from the hydroxyl group on C4, facilitating the cleavage that releases glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P or GAP).

      • The enzyme then helps form another protonated Schiff base.

      • After hydrolysis, dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) is released, completing the reaction.

      • The enzyme Aldolase uses Aspartate and Lysine residues to facilitate the reaction through acid-base catalysis.

  5. Step 5: Isomerization of DHAP to G3P

    • Reaction: Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) is isomerized to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P).

    • Reversible reaction.

    • Enzyme: Triose Phosphate Isomerase (TPI/TIM).

    • Key Concepts:

      • Isomerization: Converts DHAP to G3P, allowing all the initial glucose molecules to proceed through the payoff phase.

    • Significance: This ensures that both products from step 4 can continue through the remaining steps of glycolysis.

    • If enzyme mutates, can’t convert. Glycolysis won’t stop (half can still continue).

    • TPI stabilises intermediate product through loop structure that forms H-bonds with phosphate group. Prevents substrate from leaving before reaction is complete.

    • TPI deficiency (autosomal recessive inheritance):

      • Hemolytic anemia, caused by the rupture of red blood cells, leading to early-onset anemia.

      • Lack of ATP in granulocytes (white blood cells), resulting in poor immune function and increased susceptibility to illness.

      • Various neurological disorders, accompanied by neuromuscular defects. The reason is that the brain and nerves require a large amount of ATP to function properly.

      • Cardiomyopathy or myocarditis (heart muscle diseases).

      • Mutations are extremely rare, and most affected individuals die during childhood (early mortality).

    • Reaction Mechanism:

      • In the enzyme’s active site, there are glutamic acid (Glu) and histidine (His) residues. First, Glu (acting as a base) takes a proton (Ha⁺) from the C-1 of DHAP (dihydroxyacetone phosphate), and His (acting as an acid) donates a proton (Hc⁺) to DHAP, forming a C=C double bond, which results in the formation of an enediol intermediate.

      • After a series of complex intermediate steps, His takes back a proton (Hb⁺) from the hydroxyl group on C-1, converting the product into an aldehyde. Glu then donates a proton (Hd⁺) to C-2 of the enediol intermediate, converting the double bond back into a single bond. This results in the formation of G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate), which is recognized by glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase in Step 6 of glycolysis.

      • (The carbon numbering from the original glucose molecule can be tracked: 1, 2, 3 correspond to 6, 5, 4 in the final product.)

      • This reaction involves acid-base catalysis.

II. Payoff Phase

  1. Step 6: Oxidation of G3P

    • Reaction: Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P) is oxidized and phosphorylated to 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (1,3-BPG).

    • Reversible reaction.

    • Enzyme: Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH).

    • Key Concepts:

      • Oxidation: Involves the loss of electrons from a molecule.

      • NAD+: A coenzyme that acts as an electron carrier, accepting electrons from G3P and becoming reduced to NADH.

      • Coupled Reaction: The energy released during G3P oxidation is used to attach a phosphate group to 1,3-BPG.

    • Significance: Generates the first high-energy molecule (1,3-BPG) and NADH, a crucial electron carrier in cellular respiration.

    • GAPDH Mechanism:

      • The sulfur from cysteine in the enzyme’s active site (Step 1) and the oxygen from the phosphate group (Step 5) act as nucleophiles, successively attacking the carbonyl carbon of the aldehyde. This takes advantage of the reactivity of the carbonyl bond to drive the reaction forward.

      • NAD⁺ oxidizes the substrate, forming a thioester intermediate, and is reduced to NADH.

      • The NADH that forms leaves the enzyme, and another NAD⁺ from the cytoplasm fills the vacated position.

      • The phosphate group in Step 5 comes from inorganic phosphate, not from ATP.

    • In Step 7, 1,3-BPG is converted into 3-phosphoglycerate (3PG), producing 1 ATP. This step can be viewed as the transfer of an inorganic phosphate to ADP to form ATP. This is a crucial step in the energy payoff phase, where ATP is generated.

      • (Supplement - 113): The thioester bond formed in this reaction is highly reactive and unstable, making it more susceptible to nucleophilic attack by phosphate. The enzyme catalyzes the formation of this thioester intermediate to facilitate phosphate transfer.

      • The NAD⁺ is shown as dissociable (represented by a dashed line) in the enzyme mechanism, meaning it can easily associate and dissociate from the enzyme.

  2. Step 7: Generation of the First ATP

    • Reaction: 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (1,3-BPG) donates a phosphate group to ADP, forming ATP and 3-phosphoglycerate (3PG).

    • Reversible reaction despite being energetically favorable.

    • Enzyme: Phosphoglycerate Kinase.

    • Key Concepts:

      • Substrate-Level Phosphorylation: ATP synthesis directly from a high-energy substrate molecule (1,3-BPG).

    • Significance: The first ATP-generating step in glycolysis.

  3. Step 8: Isomerization of 3PG to 2PG

    • Reaction: 3-Phosphoglycerate (3PG) is isomerized to 2-phosphoglycerate (2PG).

    • Reversible reaction.

    • Enzyme: Phosphoglycerate Mutase.

    • Key Concepts:

      • Purpose: This rearrangement prepares the molecule for the subsequent dehydration step.

  4. Step 9: Dehydration of 2PG

    • Reaction: 2-Phosphoglycerate (2PG) undergoes dehydration, forming phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP).

    • Reversible reaction.

    • Enzyme: Enolase.

    • Requires Mg2+ as a cofactor for stabilization.

    • Key Concepts:

      • Dehydration: The removal of a water molecule, creating a double bond that increases the molecule's energy level.

    • Significance: Generates the second high-energy molecule (PEP), preparing for the final ATP synthesis step.

  5. Step 10: Generation of the Second ATP

    • Reaction: Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) donates its phosphate group to ADP, forming ATP and pyruvate.

    • Irreversible reaction due to the highly favorable conversion of PEP to pyruvate.

    • Enzyme: Pyruvate Kinase.

    • Requires K+ and Mg2+ as cofactors.

  • Key Concepts:

    • Substrate-Level Phosphorylation: The second ATP-generating step through direct phosphate transfer.

Glycolysis: Summary

  • Overall Reaction: Glucose + 2 NAD+ + 2 ADP + 2 Pi → 2 Pyruvate + 2 NADH + 2 H+ + 2 ATP + 2 H2O

  • Net ATP Gain: 2 ATP molecules per glucose molecule.

  • Key Regulatory Steps: Steps 1, 3, and 10 are irreversible and tightly regulated to control the flux of glucose through the pathway.

  • These regulatory steps ensure that ATP levels remain relatively constant within the cell.

Regulation of Glycolysis

Short-term Regulation: Allosteric mechanisms provide rapid control, primarily through feedback inhibition by ATP and citrate on key enzymes like PFK-1.

Long-term Regulation: Hormonal control through insulin and glucagon regulates enzyme synthesis and degradation to modulate glycolysis rates over a longer period.

Feeder Pathways for Glycolysis: Detailed Study Notes

These study notes expand upon the previous discussion of glycolysis by exploring the feeder pathways that allow various carbohydrates to enter this essential metabolic process.

Introduction

  • While glucose is the primary substrate for glycolysis, other carbohydrates, including glycogen, starch, disaccharides, and other monosaccharides, can also be utilized for energy production. These carbohydrates must first be converted into intermediates that can enter the glycolytic pathway. These conversion processes are known as feeder pathways.

Metabolism of Glucose Derivatives: Glycogen and Starch

  • Glycogen and starch, both homopolymers of glucose, represent the primary storage forms of glucose in animals and plants, respectively. The breakdown of these polysaccharides into glucose monomers that can enter glycolysis involves two key steps:

  1. Amylase Hydrolysis:

    • Salivary α-amylase, an enzyme present in saliva, initiates the digestion of starch and glycogen by hydrolyzing the α(1→4) glycosidic linkages within these polysaccharides.

    • This hydrolysis yields shorter chains of glucose units, including dextrins, maltose (a disaccharide), and ultimately, glucose.

  2. Phosphorylation:

    • Glycogen (or starch) phosphorylase catalyzes the release of glucose-1-phosphate (G1P) from the non-reducing ends of glycogen or starch molecules.

    • Phosphoglucomutase, an isomerase, then converts G1P to glucose-6-phosphate (G6P).

    • G6P can then enter directly into glycolysis.

Key Points:

  • The phosphate group required for the formation of G6P during glycogen/starch breakdown comes directly from inorganic phosphate (Pi) and not from ATP.

  • This difference in phosphate sources results in a net gain of 3 ATP molecules per glucose residue derived from glycogen or starch during glycolysis, as opposed to the 2 ATP molecules gained from free glucose.

Metabolism of Other Monosaccharides

Various other monosaccharides can be channeled into the glycolytic pathway through specific enzymatic reactions:

  1. D-Mannose Metabolism:

    • Hexokinase, the same enzyme that phosphorylates glucose in the first step of glycolysis, can also phosphorylate D-mannose to mannose-6-phosphate.

    • Phosphomannose isomerase then converts mannose-6-phosphate to fructose-6-phosphate (F6P), an intermediate in glycolysis.

  2. D-Galactose Metabolism:

    • Galactokinase phosphorylates D-galactose to galactose-1-phosphate (Gal-1-P), utilizing one ATP molecule.

    • Gal-1-P uridylyltransferase catalyzes a reaction between Gal-1-P and UDP-glucose, producing UDP-galactose and glucose-1-phosphate (G1P).

    • Phosphoglucomutase converts G1P to glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), which can then enter glycolysis.

    • The UDP-galactose generated in this pathway can be converted back to UDP-glucose by UDP-Gal epimerase for further use in the conversion of galactose.

  3. D-Fructose Metabolism:

    • Fructose can enter glycolysis through two distinct routes:

      • Route 1: Conversion to F6P:

        • Hexokinase, demonstrating its broad substrate specificity, can also phosphorylate D-fructose to fructose-6-phosphate (F6P).

      • Route 2: Conversion to Fructose-1-phosphate and Subsequent Reactions:

        • Fructokinase, a kinase with higher specificity for fructose, phosphorylates D-fructose to fructose-1-phosphate.

        • Fructose-1-phosphate aldolase cleaves fructose-1-phosphate into glyceraldehyde and dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP).

        • Triose kinase phosphorylates glyceraldehyde to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P), using one ATP molecule.

        • Triose phosphate isomerase converts DHAP to G3P.

Lactose Intolerance

  • Lactose, a disaccharide composed of glucose and galactose, requires the enzyme lactase for its digestion in the small intestine.

  • Lactase deficiency, or lactose intolerance, results in the inability to completely digest lactose in the small intestine.

  • Undigested lactose passes into the large intestine, where it undergoes fermentation by gut bacteria.

  • This fermentation produces gases like hydrogen, methane, and carbon dioxide, leading to symptoms such as bloating, gas, and diarrhea.

Types of Lactose Intolerance:

  • Primary: The most common type, characterized by a decline in lactase production with age.

  • Secondary: Results from damage to the small intestine due to factors like infections, inflammatory bowel disease, or certain medications.

  • Congenital: A rare genetic disorder where individuals are born without the ability to produce lactase.

  • Developmental: Occurs in premature infants as lactase production typically begins later in gestation.

Galactosemia

  • Galactosemia is a group of rare genetic disorders characterized by the inability to metabolize galactose effectively. Unlike lactose intolerance, which involves a deficiency in the digestive enzyme lactase, galactosemia stems from deficiencies in enzymes involved in the intracellular metabolism of galactose.

Causes of Galactosemia:

  • Galactosemia can arise from deficiencies in any of the three enzymes involved in galactose metabolism:

  1. Galactokinase Deficiency:

    • This deficiency leads to the accumulation of galactose in the body.

    • Some galactose is converted to galactitol, a sugar alcohol, which can build up in tissues like the lens of the eye, causing cataracts.

  2. Gal-1-P Uridylyltransferase Deficiency:

    • This is the most severe form of galactosemia, characterized by the accumulation of galactose, galactitol, and galactose-1-phosphate (Gal-1-P).

    • Symptoms typically manifest shortly after birth and can include feeding difficulties, failure to thrive, liver dysfunction, and intellectual disabilities.

    • The reason why dietary restriction of galactose does not fully alleviate symptoms in some individuals with this deficiency remains unclear.

  3. UDP-Gal-4-epimerase Deficiency:

    • This deficiency leads to the accumulation of galactose-1-phosphate and other intermediates.

    • Symptoms are generally milder than those associated with Gal-1-P uridylyltransferase deficiency and can often be managed by dietary restriction of galactose.

Types of Galactosemia:

  • Type 1: Classic galactosemia, caused by a deficiency in galactose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase.

  • Type 2: Galactokinase deficiency.

  • Type 3: Galactose epimerase deficiency.

Pyruvate's Fate:

  • Aerobic conditions: Pyruvate is oxidized to Acetyl-CoA and CO2, entering the citric acid cycle for respiration.

  • Anaerobic conditions: Pyruvate undergoes fermentation.

Fermentation:

  • Definition: The breakdown of glucose and other organic compounds under anaerobic conditions to generate energy in the form of ATP.

  • Energy yield: Fermentation produces less energy per glucose molecule compared to aerobic respiration due to the absence of the citric acid cycle. This leads to higher glucose consumption and a faster consumption rate.

Types:

  • Lactate fermentation: Occurs in oxygen-deprived muscle tissues and red blood cells (lacking mitochondria). The liver converts lactate back to glucose.

  • Ethanol fermentation: Produces CO2, ethanol, and lactate.

  • Key function: Oxidizes NADH generated during glycolysis back to NAD+, maintaining cellular NAD+ concentration and ensuring the continuation of glycolysis. (Human NAD+ concentration is around 10-5M, easily influenced by concentration changes).

Lactate Fermentation:

  • Reaction:

    • ΔG = -25.1 kJ/mol, indicating a highly favorable reaction towards lactate formation.

  • Catalyzed by lactate dehydrogenase.

  • Occurs primarily in oxygen-deprived skeletal muscles and red blood cells.

  • Importance: Maintains the balance of NAD+ and NADH in anaerobic respiration, enabling continuous ATP production in anaerobic conditions.

Comparison with Glycolysis:

  • Fermentation: Glucose → 2 Lactate + 2 ATP

  • Glycolysis: Glucose + 2 NAD+ + 2 ADP + 2 Pi → 2 Pyruvate + 2 NADH + 2 H+ + 2 ATP + 2 H2O

Cori Cycle:

  • An inter-organ communication system between muscles and the liver involving glucose and lactate conversion.

  • During intense exercise with oxygen depletion, muscle cells undergo anaerobic respiration via lactate fermentation, leading to lactate accumulation.

  • Lactate travels via blood to the liver, where gluconeogenesis converts it back to glucose at the cost of 6 ATP. This glucose is then transported back to the muscles via blood.

  • Each Cori Cycle results in a net loss of 4 ATP, making it an energy-intensive process typically utilized in emergencies or life-threatening situations.

Ethanol Fermentation:

  • Reaction:

    • Pyruvate decarboxylase removes a carboxyl group from pyruvate, forming acetaldehyde. This step is irreversible.

    • Alcohol dehydrogenase catalyzes the reduction of acetaldehyde to ethanol using NADH.

  • Species specificity: Not all organisms possess pyruvate decarboxylase and alcohol dehydrogenase, hence ethanol fermentation is not universal.

Reverse reaction:

  • While the reverse reaction can produce pyruvate from ethanol, humans lack pyruvate decarboxylase and only have alcohol dehydrogenase, limiting the reaction to the production of acetaldehyde from ethanol.

Alcohol metabolism:

  • Discomfort from alcohol consumption is due to acetaldehyde, a product of alcohol oxidation.

  • Acetaldehyde is metabolized to acetate in the liver by acetaldehyde dehydrogenase.

  • Individuals with a deficiency in this enzyme, like some East Asians, have lower alcohol tolerance.

Impact of alcohol on glycolysis:

  • Alcohol consumption reduces glycolysis efficiency because ethanol metabolism consumes NAD+, impacting step 6 of glycolysis.

Role of Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) in Pyruvate Decarboxylation:

  • TPP carbanion acts as a nucleophile, attacking the carbonyl group of pyruvate.

  • Decarboxylation leads to the formation of a carbanion stabilized by the thiazolium ring.

  • Protonation of this carbanion forms hydroxyethyl TPP, which then releases acetaldehyde.

  • A proton is released, regenerating the original TPP carbanion.

  • TPP is a derivative of thiamine (vitamin B1).

Applications of Fermentation:

  • Food preservation and enhancement: Historically, fermentation was an accidental discovery for preserving food. It alters food texture and composition, often making it more palatable. Example: Fermenting cabbage into kimchi.

  • Industrial applications: Fermentation has broad industrial uses, transforming inexpensive carbohydrates into valuable organic compounds. Example: Biofuel production.

  • Recycling fermentation products: Products of fermentation can be recycled and fermented again, but this process is inefficient. Example: Galactose can be converted back to glucose in the liver, but with a net loss of 4 ATP molecules.

Acetyl-CoA Production and Oxidation: A Detailed Examination

These notes will cover the generation of Acetyl-CoA and its subsequent oxidation in the citric acid cycle, drawing on information from the provided source.

Acetyl-CoA Generation: A Crucial Step in Aerobic Respiration

  • Acetyl-CoA formation serves as a critical link between glycolysis and the citric acid cycle, bridging the gap between glucose breakdown and energy production in the presence of oxygen.

Overview of the Reaction

  1. Overall Reaction:

    • Pyruvate + CoA + NAD+ → Acetyl-CoA + CO2 + NADH

  2. Key Enzyme: The reaction is catalyzed by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDH complex or PDC).

  3. Irreversible and Energy-Releasing: This oxidative decarboxylation reaction is irreversible and releases energy.

  4. Enzymes and Cofactors: The process requires the coordinated action of three enzymes and five cofactors.

The Pyruvate Dehydrogenase Complex: Structure and Function

  1. Structure:

    • Three Enzyme Subunits: The PDH complex comprises three distinct enzymes: pyruvate dehydrogenase (E1), dihydrolipoyl transacetylase (E2), and dihydrolipoyl dehydrogenase (E3).

    • Non-Covalent Interactions: These subunits, encoded by different genes, are tightly bound together by non-covalent interactions, making it impossible to purify them individually.

  2. Key Features:

    • Multi-enzyme Complex: The PDC functions as a multi-enzyme complex, facilitating efficient substrate channeling.

    • Substrate Channeling: Intermediate products remain bound to the enzyme surface throughout the reaction sequence, minimizing side reactions and enhancing efficiency.

Regulation of PDC Activity:

  • ATP Levels and Phosphorylation: High ATP concentrations activate a kinase that phosphorylates a serine residue on the E1 subunit, reducing PDH complex activity.

  1. Step-by-Step Reaction Mechanism:

    • The conversion of pyruvate to Acetyl-CoA by the PDH complex involves a series of five interconnected steps:

    1. Step 1: Decarboxylation and TPP Binding:

      • Pyruvate binds to the thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) cofactor within the E1 subunit's active site.

      • Decarboxylation occurs, releasing CO2 and forming hydroxyethyl-TPP.

      • This step, often the rate-limiting step, provides the energy needed to drive the entire reaction.

    2. Step 2: Acetyl Group Transfer to Lipoamide:

      • The hydroxyethyl group is transferred from TPP to the oxidized lipoamide arm of the E2 subunit.

      • This transfer forms an acyl-lipoyllysine intermediate and regenerates the TPP cofactor.

    3. Step 3: Acetyl-CoA Formation and Release:

      • Coenzyme A (CoA) enters the active site of E2.

      • The acetyl group is transferred from acyl-lipoyllysine to CoA, forming Acetyl-CoA.

      • Acetyl-CoA is released from the complex.

      • The lipoamide arm is left in its reduced form.

    4. Step 4: Lipoamide Reoxidation by FAD:

      • The reduced lipoamide arm on E2 must be reoxidized for the cycle to continue.

      • FAD, tightly bound to the E3 subunit, accepts two electrons (and two protons) from the reduced lipoamide, becoming FADH2.

    5. Step 5: FADH2 Reoxidation by NAD+:

      • FADH2, tightly bound to E3, cannot directly transfer its electrons to the electron transport chain.

      • NAD+ acts as a mobile electron carrier, accepting electrons from FADH2.

      • This transfer regenerates FAD and produces NADH, which can enter the electron transport chain.

Advantages of a Multienzyme Complex:

  • The organization of the PDH complex as a multienzyme complex offers several advantages:

  1. Enhanced Efficiency: The close proximity of enzymes within the complex allows for rapid substrate channeling, where the product of one reaction becomes the substrate of the next without diffusing away.

  2. Minimized Side Reactions: By keeping intermediates bound to the enzyme surface, the PDH complex minimizes the potential for side reactions.

  3. Coordinated Control: The activities of all three enzyme subunits are tightly coupled, ensuring a coordinated flow of substrates and products through the reaction sequence.

Arsenic Poisoning: Disrupting Energy Production

  1. Arsenic Binding: Arsenic compounds can form covalent bonds with sulfhydryl groups (-SH) commonly found in proteins.

  2. Lipoamide Inactivation: When arsenic binds to the lipoamide cofactor's sulfhydryl groups within the E2 subunit of the PDH complex, lipoamide loses its ability to participate in redox reactions.

  3. Metabolic Consequences: This inactivation disrupts the PDH complex's function, halting Acetyl-CoA production and ultimately blocking the citric acid cycle, leading to cellular toxicity.

Citric Acid Cycle: Oxidizing Acetyl-CoA for Energy

  • The citric acid cycle, also known as the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle or Krebs cycle, is a central metabolic pathway that oxidizes Acetyl-CoA, generating energy-rich molecules.

Overview of the Citric Acid Cycle

  1. Cellular Location: Unlike glycolysis, which occurs in the cytoplasm, the citric acid cycle takes place within the mitochondria, the cell's energy powerhouse.

  2. Oxygen Dependence: The citric acid cycle is an aerobic process, requiring oxygen for the reoxidation of electron carriers (NADH and FADH2) generated during the cycle.

  3. Overall Reaction:

    • Acetyl-CoA + 3NAD+ + FAD + GDP + Pi + 2H2O → 2 CO2 + CoA-SH + 3 NADH + FADH2 + GTP + 3 H+

  4. Key Points:

    • Dehydrogenases and Electron Carriers: Four of the eight steps in the citric acid cycle are catalyzed by dehydrogenases, enzymes that transfer electrons from substrates to electron carriers (NAD+ or FAD).

    • CO2 Release: Two carbon atoms enter the cycle as Acetyl-CoA and are released as CO2 in two oxidative decarboxylation steps.

    • GTP Production: One molecule of GTP (equivalent in energy to ATP) is produced per cycle.

    • Water Consumption and Production: Two water molecules are consumed, and three are produced in each cycle.

    • Cyclic Nature: The citric acid cycle is a true cycle, regenerating oxaloacetate (OAA), the initial four-carbon acceptor molecule, in the final step.

  5. Enzymes of the Citric Acid Cycle:

    • Citrate synthase

    • Aconitase

    • Isocitrate dehydrogenase

    • α-Ketoglutarate dehydrogenase (similar to PDC, sharing the same E3 subunit)

    • Succinyl-CoA synthetase

    • Succinate dehydrogenase (uses FAD)

    • Fumarase

    • Malate dehydrogenase

  6. Synthetase vs. Synthase: Synthetases utilize ATP (or another NTP) as an energy source for synthetic reactions, while synthases do not require NTP hydrolysis.

Step 1: Citrate Synthase - An Irreversible Condensation

  1. Reaction:

    • Oxaloacetate (OAA), a four-carbon molecule, condenses with Acetyl-CoA (two carbons) to form citrate (six carbons), releasing CoA-SH.

  2. Key Features:

    • Irreversible: This highly exergonic reaction has a large negative free energy change (ΔG0 = -32.2 kJ/mol), making it essentially irreversible under physiological conditions.

    • Ordered Binding and Induced Fit: OAA binds first, inducing a conformational change in the enzyme that creates the binding site for Acetyl-CoA.

    • Regulation: Citrate synthase is an important control point in the citric acid cycle, regulated by substrate availability, product inhibition (citrate and ATP), and cellular energy status.

  3. Structural Insights:

    • Open and Closed Conformations: The enzyme exists in open and closed forms.

    • OAA Binding Induces Closure: Binding of OAA triggers a large conformational change, closing the enzyme around the substrates and facilitating catalysis.

Step 2: Aconitase - Isomerization via Dehydration/Hydration

  1. Reaction:

    • Aconitase catalyzes the reversible isomerization of citrate (six carbons) to isocitrate (six carbons).

  2. Mechanism:

    • Dehydration: Citrate is first dehydrated to form cis-aconitate, an intermediate with a double bond.

    • Hydration: Water is added back to cis-aconitate, forming isocitrate.

  3. Iron-Sulfur Cluster:

    • Stereochemical specificity: Aconitase exhibits stereochemical specificity, recognizing and catalyzing reactions with specific stereochemistry.

    • Iron-sulfur cluster cofactor: Aconitase binds to an iron-sulfur cluster ([Fe4S4]²⁺), which aids in citrate binding and water removal during catalysis. The ferrous ion (Fe²⁺) stabilizes the structure, and three iron atoms are bonded to cysteine residues in aconitase.

  4. Prochiral center recognition: Aconitase can recognize prochiral centers and treat them as chiral centers during catalysis, allowing it to treat symmetric molecules as if they were asymmetric.

  5. Isotope Labeling Studies:

    • Experiment observation: Labeling Acetyl-CoA with radioactive 14C shows that the subsequent CO2 produced does not exhibit radioactivity.

    • Carboxyl group removal: Only the lower carboxyl group of labeled citrate is removed in Step 4, while the carboxyl group from Acetyl-CoA remains intact.

    • Double bond formation: During Step 2 (dehydration), the double bond forms between the two carbons originally from oxaloacetate (OAA).

    • Decarboxylation result: In Step 4, the decarboxylation process does not remove the carboxyl group contributed by Acetyl-CoA.

Subsequent Steps and Overall Regulation

  • The remaining steps of the citric acid cycle involve further oxidation and rearrangement reactions, ultimately regenerating oxaloacetate (OAA) to allow for continued cycling. These steps, coupled with the reactions of the electron transport chain, drive ATP synthesis through oxidative phosphorylation. The citric acid cycle is tightly regulated at multiple points, ensuring efficient energy production in response to cellular needs.

Citric Acid Cycle Notes

Step 3: Isocitrate Dehydrogenase (Irreversible)

  1. Overall reaction:

    • Isocitrate → α-Ketoglutarate + CO2

    • Two carbon atoms enter the citric acid cycle in the form of Acetyl-CoA and are released in the form of CO₂ during oxidative decarboxylation in step 3 (Isocitrate dehydrogenase) and step 4 (α-Ketoglutarate Dehydrogenase).

  • There are two isoforms of the enzyme Isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) used in this reaction, which use NAD+ and NADP+ as cofactors respectively.

  • NAD+ is reduced to NADH (or NADP+ is reduced to NADPH), oxidizing Isocitrate to Oxalosuccinate. After oxidative decarboxylation to release CO2, an unstable structure is formed.

  • The unstable structure quickly forms α-Ketoglutarate.

Bioenergetics and Biochemical Thermodynamics

These study notes, based on the provided source material, will guide you through the intricacies of bioenergetics and biochemical thermodynamics.

I. Introduction: Glucose and Energy Imbalances

  1. The Versatility of Glucose

    • Structural Polymer Synthesis: Glucose serves as a building block for structural polymers like the extracellular matrix and cell walls.

    • Redox Reactions: In redox reactions, glucose acts as a reducing agent, neutralizing harmful substances like peroxides and free radicals.

    • Pentose Phosphate Pathway: Glucose oxidation via the pentose phosphate pathway generates NADPH (a reducing agent) and ribose 5-phosphate (a nucleotide precursor).

    • Energy Storage: Animals store energy as glycogen, synthesized from glucose. Plants store energy as starch and sucrose, also derived from glucose.

    • Glycolysis Substrate: Glucose is the primary substrate for glycolysis, a crucial energy-producing pathway.

    • Blood Glucose Homeostasis: Glucose maintains stable blood sugar levels. During extreme conditions, when glycogen stores are depleted, gluconeogenesis converts pyruvate to glucose.

    • Glycosylation: Glycosylation, the addition of sugar molecules to other molecules (like drugs or metabolites of hemoglobin), enhances their water solubility.

  2. Bioenergetics and Disease

    • Definition: Bioenergetics explores the thermodynamics of biochemical reactions, focusing on energy transformations within living organisms.

    • Energy Imbalances and Disease: Disruptions in energy balance can lead to various diseases:

      • Marasmus: This severe malnutrition results from inadequate energy intake, leading to the breakdown of fat and muscle tissue, causing emaciation. Long-term starvation contributes to this nutritional deficiency.

      • Obesity: Excessive food intake or slow metabolism can cause an energy surplus, leading to fat storage and obesity. Excess glycogen storage also plays a role.

  3. ATP: Overcoming Energy Barriers

    • Metabolic Reactions: Metabolic reactions, often facilitated by enzymes, require ATP to overcome energy barriers and proceed efficiently.

    • Glycogen Metabolism: Maintaining a balance between glucose and glycogen is crucial.

  4. Mitochondria and ATP Synthesis

    • Energy Conversion: This section focuses on the importance of energy and the mitochondria's role in converting products of glycolysis and the TCA cycle into ATP.

II. Bioenergetics, Free Energy, and Chemical Energy

  1. Laws of Thermodynamics

    • First Law: Energy is conserved in a closed system, meaning it cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.

    • Second Law: Spontaneous reactions tend towards increased entropy (disorder). Reducing entropy requires energy input, often in the form of ATP.

  2. Free Energy (ΔG) Drives Reactions

    • Definition: Free energy (ΔG) represents the portion of a reaction's energy change that can perform work.

    • Formula: ΔG = ΔE - TΔS (where ΔE is the change in internal energy, T is temperature, and ΔS is the change in entropy). Note that while ΔG is formally defined as ΔH - TΔS (where ΔH represents the change in enthalpy), in biological systems, ΔH and ΔE are often quite similar, hence the use of ΔG = ΔE - TΔS.

    • Driving Force: In biological systems, both anabolic (building up) and catabolic (breaking down) reactions utilize free energy.

    • Example: The hydrolysis of ATP (ATP → ADP + Pi) has a ΔG° of -30.5 kJ/mol. This means that the breakdown of 1 M ATP to 1 M ADP and 1 M Pi can release 30.5 kJ of energy to drive other reactions. This energy transfer from ATP's covalent bonds helps overcome activation energy barriers.

  3. Chemical Energy

    • Breaking Bonds: Organisms extract energy by breaking chemical bonds, converting it into usable forms like ATP.

    • Example: Glucose, through glycolysis and the TCA cycle (aerobic respiration), releases energy. This energy is then harnessed by the electron transport chain to generate ATP, powering various cellular processes.

III. Coupling: Linking Energy-Releasing and Energy-Requiring Reactions

  1. Catabolism and Anabolism

    • Metabolic Processes: These are collectively referred to as metabolism.

  1. Coupling Reactions

    • Energy Transfer: Anabolic and catabolic reactions are often coupled. Endergonic (energy-consuming) reactions require energy input from exergonic (energy-releasing) reactions.

    • Example: Consider the reaction A + C → B + D + Heat, where A → B is exergonic, releasing free energy. C → D, being endergonic, wouldn't occur spontaneously. The energy released by A → B drives C → D. The free energy released by A → B must exceed the energy required by C → D. Some energy is conserved as chemical energy (e.g., ATP production), while the rest dissipates as heat, adhering to the laws of thermodynamics. This process also follows the second law, tending towards greater entropy (heat being the most disordered form of energy).

  2. High-Energy Intermediates: Biological systems often use high-energy intermediates, primarily ATP, to facilitate energy transfer between reactions.

IV. ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)

  1. Roles of ATP

    • Hydrogen Carrier: Many biochemical reactions in the body, such as Glycolysis and certain reactions in the TCA cycle, proceed through the "Coupling of dehydrogenation and hydrogenation" (Hydrogen transfer) to carry out oxidation-reduction.

    • Reaction: AH2 + B → I → A + BH2

    • (113 Supplement) Gaining H atoms is reduction; losing H atoms is oxidation.

    • When the reactant AH2 is oxidized, it transfers Hydrogen to a Carrier, resulting in the Carrier carrying Hydrogen and providing a source of reducing power. The Carrier with Hydrogen can then act as a reducing agent in the next oxidation-reduction reaction, transferring Hydrogen to reactant B and reducing it to BH2.

    • Example:

      • NAD+ → NADH + H+

      • FAD → FADH2

  2. Organisms commonly capture energy using Carrier molecules.

    • As depicted in the diagram below, a portion of the free energy released during the reaction A → B is captured by a low-energy Carrier/intermediate (E), transforming it into a high-energy Carrier (~E). This high-energy Carrier then transfers the energy to the endergonic reaction C → D.

    • The most frequently used high-energy Carrier (~E) in living organisms is ATP. In aerobic respiration, FADH2 or NADH act as high-energy Carriers (~E) in processes like Glycolysis and the TCA cycle, which are subsequently converted into ATP through the electron transport chain.

    • (98 Supplement) The advantage of using Carrier molecules for energy transfer is that the structure of the Carrier itself is independent of A, B, C, and D, allowing it to freely transfer energy between various reactions.

  3. Structure of ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate)

    • The structure of ATP comprises Adenine, a 5-carbon sugar (Ribose), and 3 Phosphate groups.

    • The combination of Adenine and Ribose is called Adenosine.

    • The first Phosphate group attached to Ribose is termed α-phosphate, the second is β-phosphate, and the third is γ-phosphate. High-energy covalent bonds exist between α and β, and between β and γ.

    • The phosphate bonds between Phosphate groups are the high-energy sites of ATP. When a bond breaks, a phosphate group (often the γ-phosphate) is released along with energy, converting ATP into ADP (Adenosine diphosphate). Further bond breakage releases another phosphate group (β-phosphate), resulting in AMP (Adenosine monophosphate) with only one phosphate group remaining.

  4. High-Energy Phosphates Play a Central Role in Energy Capture and Transfer

    • In biochemical reactions, the energy contained in phosphate bonds varies depending on the substance. Therefore, the transfer of phosphate groups also represents energy transfer, enabling biochemical reactions to be driven. Based on their energy level compared to ATP, they are classified as High-energy Phosphates and Low-energy Phosphates.

  5. High-Energy Phosphates (or Phosphagens)

    • During metabolism, the free energy released upon hydrolysis of High-energy Phosphates is greater than that released by ATP hydrolysis to ADP. Therefore, the hydrolysis of High-energy Phosphates can transfer Pi to ADP, generating ATP.

    • Examples of High-energy Phosphate compounds:

      • Phosphoenolpyruvate

      • Carbamoyl phosphate: Found in nucleotide metabolism, its energy level difference is also higher than that of ATP conversion to ADP.

      • Creatine phosphate: Its energy level is higher than that of ATP hydrolysis to ADP. In higher vertebrates, when ATP concentration is high, Creatine phosphate is formed by attaching Phosphate to Creatine, storing excess energy. When ATP is insufficient, the higher energy of Creatine phosphate hydrolysis allows the transfer of the phosphate group to ADP, producing ATP.

    • (112 Supplement) Creatine acts like a battery within cells, regulating the balance of ATP and ADP concentrations.

    • ATP → AMP + PPi: ATP loses 2 phosphate groups, and the energy level of the phosphate bond between α and β is higher than that released between β and γ. The released energy can convert ADP into ATP (although this usage is less efficient). Therefore, it is classified as a High-energy phosphate. This reaction is involved in fat metabolism.

    • (98 Supplement) 1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate: When 1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate converts to 3-Phosphoglycerate, the high-energy phosphate bond at C-1 allows ADP to become ATP.

  6. Low-Energy Phosphates

    • The energy released from bond breakage in Low-energy Phosphate compounds is smaller, insufficient to add the resulting Pi to ADP and generate ATP. Therefore, Pi is released freely; during reactions, it needs to combine with a phosphate group from ATP hydrolysis (increasing the energy level).

    • Examples of Low-energy Phosphate compounds:

      • Glucose 1-phosphate: Hydrolysis of the phosphate group at C-1 cannot drive ADP conversion to ATP, hence its classification as a Low-energy phosphate.

      • PPi

      • Fructose 6-phosphate

      • Glucose 6-phosphate: Reactant in step 2 of glycolysis.

      • Glycerol 3-phosphate: Involved in fat metabolism.

V. High-Energy Phosphates Act as the "Energy Currency" of the Cell

  1. ATP is continuously consumed and regenerated.

  2. The ATP/ADP cycle links reactions that consume ~P with reactions that generate ~P.

  3. Energy Conservation (or Energy Capture):

  4. Each NADH generates 2.5-3 ATP through Oxidative Phosphorylation, while each FADH2 generates approximately 1.5-2 ATP. However, the actual utilization efficiency must consider the specific cell type where the process occurs.

  5. Three primary energy sources for Energy Conservation:

    • Oxidative Phosphorylation (requires oxygen): Utilizes the energy released during electron transfer in Oxidative Phosphorylation to generate a large amount of ATP from NADH and FADH2 produced in reactions such as the TCA cycle and Glycolysis. This is the primary source of ATP production: 28 ATP per glucose molecule.

    • Glycolysis: During the payoff phase, a total of 4 ATP are released from reactions involving Phosphoenolpyruvate and 1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate. 2 ATP per glucose molecule (2 ATP are consumed during the investment phase).

    • Citric Acid Cycle (TCA cycle): For example, the conversion of Succinyl-CoA to Succinate.

      • 2 GTP per glucose molecule (1 GTP per Acetyl-CoA).

  6. Phosphagens can serve as storage sites for High-energy phosphates.

    • (113 Supplement) Usually involve enzymes.

    • Creatine phosphate: Found in the muscles, heart, sperm, and brain of vertebrates. When energy is abundant and the ATP/ADP ratio is high, the concentration of Creatine phosphate increases to store High-energy phosphate. When the ATP/ADP ratio is low, High-energy phosphate is transferred to ADP through the action of Creatine kinase, generating ATP.

    • (104 Supplement) High-energy phosphates have two meanings: They can refer to high-energy phosphate bonds or compounds carrying these bonds. In this context, it refers to the former.

    • Arginine phosphate: Found in the muscles of invertebrates. Storing energy involves phosphorylation of an amino acid side chain.

    • (98 Supplement) In addition to energy transfer, ATP can also phosphorylate proteins within cells, causing changes in protein activity or conformation, thereby transmitting signals or regulating gene expression.

VI. Thermodynamic Significance of ATP

  1. Importance of ATP: Enabling thermodynamically unfavorable biochemical reactions to proceed.

    • In certain biochemical reactions, ATP first phosphorylates reactants, transferring the energy from phosphate bond to low-energy reactants. This elevates the energy level of the reactants, facilitating their progression from a high energy state to a lower energy state, tending towards maximum entropy (Second Law of Thermodynamics), allowing downstream reactions to proceed smoothly.

    • These reactions can be either endergonic or exergonic.

  2. Examples of Reactions Involving ATP:

    • Steps 1 and 3 of Glycolysis utilize ATP to increase energy levels, aiding in the progression of Glycolysis (e.g., Step 1: ATP + Glucose → ADP + Glucose-6-Phosphate).

    • Glucose metabolism only requires the energy from the phosphate bond between β and γ. However, the metabolism of long-chain fatty acids requires the energy from the phosphate bond between α and β: Attaching the Fatty acid to Acetyl-CoA to form R-CO-SCoA is necessary for entry into the TCA cycle for metabolism.

    • ATP + CoA-SH + R-COOH → AMP + PPi + R-CO-ScoA

  3. Conversion between ATP/ADP/AMP (regulated by enzymes):

    • Taking the AMP produced in the aforementioned "long-chain fatty acid metabolism" reaction as an example, one AMP is converted into ATP by two ATP molecules under the action of Adenylyl kinase, generating two ADP molecules (see point 2 below). Meanwhile, PPi is converted into two Pi molecules by Inorganic pyrophosphatase.

    • The conversion of 1 AMP to ADP (enzyme: Adenylyl Kinase) consumes one ATP. The subsequent conversion of ADP to ATP consumes another ATP, resulting in a total consumption of 2 ATP and the generation of one ATP (from AMP conversion) and two ADP (from two ATP conversions).

    • (113 Supplement) In addition to the energy sources mentioned above, another potential energy source is from the citric acid cycle and other metabolic pathways.

    • Why doesn't it consume only one ATP and generate two ADP (one from ATP and one from AMP conversion)? This is due to enzyme specificity. (Simply put, under the action of this enzyme, AMP phosphorylation must proceed all the way to ATP).

    • The reaction AMP + PPi → ATP does not occur directly because PPi is immediately hydrolyzed into two Pi by Inorganic pyrophosphatase, ensuring the reaction proceeds unidirectionally.

    • AMP acts as a metabolic signal. High AMP concentration indicates energy deficiency in tissues. In this situation, there is insufficient ADP to generate ATP (Catabolic reaction) because most phosphorylation reactions (such as Glycolysis) require ADP as a reactant, not AMP. Therefore, excess AMP triggers Adenylyl kinase activation to facilitate ADP regeneration, further synthesizing ATP.

  4. (105 Supplement) Formation of Nucleoside Triphosphates

    • Apart from the aforementioned pathways, Nucleoside diphosphate (NDP) kinase can convert UDP, GDP, and CDP into high-energy Nucleoside triphosphates (NTP): UTP, GTP, and CTP.

    • The conversion of Nucleoside monophosphates (NMP) to NDPs is catalyzed by NMP kinase. Therefore, Adenylyl kinase can be considered a specialized NMP kinase, converting AMP to ADP.
